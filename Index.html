The Fight Against Food Wastage and Its Link to Climate Change

By: [Reagan Onsongo]

Understanding the Issue

Food wastage is not just a social issue—it is a major environmental crisis that significantly contributes to climate change.Globally, nearly one-third of all food produced is wasted, amounting to around 1.3 billion tons annually, according to the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO). Shockingly, if food waste were a country, it would rank as the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases after the United States and China.

The Link to Climate Change

The connection between food wastage and climate change lies in the resources required to produce and dispose of food.Agriculture uses vast amounts of water, land, and energy, while discarded food decomposes in landfills, releasing methane—a greenhouse gas over 25 times more potent than carbon dioxide. For instance, research shows that wasted food is responsible for 8-10% of global greenhouse gas emissions, exacerbating the climate crisis.

Real-Time Data

Real-time data highlights the urgency of this issue. For example, during 2024, food wastage in developed countries like the United States reached over 100 kilograms per person, while the global average remained at 74 kilograms per person. Meanwhile, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) states that reducing food loss and waste could cut emissions by 4.5 gigatons annually by 2030.

Call to Action

To combat food wastage, we must take decisive action. This includes better food storage, redistributing surplus food, and raising awareness about sustainable consumption. By addressing food wastage, we not only reduce environmental harm but also take a vital step toward a fairer, more sustainable future for all.
